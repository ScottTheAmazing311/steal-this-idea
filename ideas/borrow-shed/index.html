The Problem

Every tool. One project. Then silence.

Homeownership comes with an endless parade of specialized tools. A sander for one dresser. A tiller for one garden bed. A leaf blower you researched for hours, only to realize you bought the corded version. Each purchase runs $100 to $500, and most of those tools sit idle for months or years after a single weekend of use.

Worse, the high cost of entry keeps homeowners from starting projects at all. You skip the laminate flooring because you do not own a cutter. You let the lawn go rough because a riding mower feels absurd for a quarter-acre lot. The tools exist, just not in your garage.

The Solution

One shed. Your whole neighborhood. Every tool you need.

Borrow installs a shared, smart-locked tool shed right in your neighborhood. It is stocked with premium power tools, lawn equipment, and specialty gear, all managed through a simple app.

1

A neighbor hosts the shed

Your local Borrow Manager volunteers their property and earns a revenue share for keeping the community running.

2

Members check out tools via the app

Browse available inventory, scan the QR code, and the smart lock opens. Snap a photo of the tool's condition on the way out and on return.

3

New gear arrives every quarter

Borrow refreshes inventory regularly, partnering with brands who want real homeowners testing their latest products.

Key Features

Built for the way neighborhoods actually work

Smart-Lock Access

Open the shed with a QR code on your phone. No keys, no scheduling calls, no awkward knocks on your neighbor's door. The app tracks every check-in and check-out automatically.

Photo Condition Tracking

Snap a photo when you check out and again when you return. Both parties are protected, and the community stays accountable without finger-pointing.

Borrow Manager Program

Host the shed on your property, recruit neighbors, and earn a cut of every membership. You are the local mayor of tools, and your free membership is just the beginning.

Curated, Rotating Inventory

New tools arrive every quarter. Brand partners supply their latest products for real-world exposure. If you fall in love with something, you can buy it outright.

Market Opportunity

80 million homeowners. One shared problem.

The U.S. tool rental market sits at roughly $7 billion a year, and the average American homeowner spends over $400 annually on tools and equipment. Most of those purchases are single-use or close to it. Borrow targets the gap between expensive ownership and inconvenient big-box rental by putting tools within walking distance.

The initial market is suburban neighborhoods with strong community ties, HOAs looking for member perks, apartment complexes where residents share common needs, and community gardens that already operate on a cooperative model.

The Business Plan

From shed to scale

Revenue Model

Subscriptions, insurance, and brand deals

Monthly memberships at $15 to $30 per household form the base. Optional $2 per-checkout rental insurance adds margin. Tool manufacturers pay to place products in sheds for real-world exposure, and a buy-to-own option lets members purchase tools they love.

Go-to-Market

Launch local, grow through community

Start in Salt Lake City metro where community ties, homeownership rates, and neighborhood culture are strong. Recruit the first 10 Borrow Managers from active neighborhood leaders, seed each shed with a standard tool kit, then expand through word of mouth and HOA partnerships.

Competitive Advantage

Hyper-local trust that big-box cannot replicate

Home Depot rentals require a trip across town and hourly fees. Borrow is subscription-based and walking distance away. The Borrow Manager model creates a distributed sales force built on real neighborhood relationships, something no corporate chain can duplicate.

Year One Milestones

From pilot to proof of concept

  • Deploy 20 Borrow sheds across 3 suburban neighborhoods
  • Reach 400 paying subscribers with 70%+ retention
  • Secure 2 brand partnerships with major tool manufacturers
Key Risks

What could go wrong

  • Tool damage, theft, or misuse driving up replacement costs
  • Difficulty reaching subscriber density per shed for profitability
  • Seasonal demand swings: lawn gear idles in winter months
