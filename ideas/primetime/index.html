PrimeTime targets millennials and Gen Z aged 21 to 40 who already spend on sports bars, escape rooms, and barcades. The secondary audience includes couples seeking date-night variety, friend groups tired of the "whose house?" debate, and families looking for weekend activities. By repurposing cheap dollar-theater real estate, buildout costs stay low while the multi-zone format drives higher per-visit spend than a traditional bar.

The Business Plan

How PrimeTime makes money and scales.

Revenue Model

  • Monthly memberships at $15 to $25/month for unlimited venue access
  • Food and beverage sales (primary margin driver at 60%+ gross margin)
  • Premium private room reservations at hourly rates
  • Special event tickets: pay-per-view fights, premiere screenings, tournaments
  • Corporate event packages and group buyouts

Go-to-Market Strategy

Launch a single flagship in a mid-size metro by acquiring or leasing a closing dollar theater. Build buzz with a founding-member presale at a discounted annual rate. Partner with local influencers and podcasters for opening-week programming events to create social proof and earned media. Expand to additional cities once the playbook is proven.

Competitive Advantage

Unlike sports bars that serve only one audience, PrimeTime captures every kind of shared viewing occasion under one roof. The membership model creates recurring revenue and a loyal community. By using cheap, underutilized theater real estate, buildout costs stay low while the multi-zone format drives higher per-visit spend than a traditional bar or cinema.

Year One Milestones

  • Secure venue lease and complete buildout within 6 months; achieve 1,000 founding members before doors open
  • Reach average nightly attendance of 150+ guests and food/bev revenue of $8,000/night by month 9
  • Host 50+ special events and hit $1.5M total revenue by end of year one

Startup Cost Estimate

$350K - $750K

Covers lease/acquisition of a closing dollar theater, A/V equipment and buildout across four zones, kitchen and bar setup, initial licensing fees, founding-member marketing campaign, and 3 months of operating runway.

Key Risks

01

Content licensing for streaming services in public venues is legally uncharted and could require costly negotiations or creative workarounds.

02

High upfront capital for lease, buildout, and A/V equipment in a business model that depends on sustained membership retention.

03

Seasonal attendance fluctuations: summer months with fewer prestige TV premieres and sports events may reduce foot traffic.
